Totems of Time

  • Object Oriented Programming, UI Design
  • Tools: Unity, Figma
  • date: Fall 2020
  • Group Project

Our game, “Totems of Time” is an adventure puzzle 3D game. The purpose is to solve all the puzzles in the ruin on a stranded island and find a raft to escape. During the gameplay, players need to solve geography puzzles. There are rotating platforms, pressure plates with raising platforms, and the most important element, time-travelling. With time travelling, the elements in the ruin change with time. Players need to use the time-travelling feature to overcome impossible missions. Find link to the prototype Windows.

Design

Initially we started to do some research on action-adventure puzzle games such as Uncharted, Tomb Raider, and others. After we gained some insights on these puzzle games, we started to think and create our own level designs while keeping our primary mechanic (time travel) in mind. During the level design progress, We Iterated on the level designs a lot since there was a lot of confusion from the players when they played the game, starting from the visual cues and hints as well as the basic mechanics of the game.

For control schemes, from the playtesting, we learned it is extremely important to have the control set to be natural. Players like to use their experience in the other games to play our game, so it is more convenient for players to use the same control as other games. One example can be using WASD for moving the character. On the other hand, it is also necessary to have keys grouped together. For example, we make the left hand mainly control WASD, and the right hand controls the mouse. In that way, players' hands do not need to move a lot on the keyboard, which allows them to have a quick reaction to the situations in the game.

Development

Since it is a 3D third-person game, the camera needs to follow the player's movement smoothly. We used a variety of features from the Cinemachine package that comes with Unity. The package has components like the free-look camera, collider avoidance, and state-driven cameras. These made it very easy for a natural-looking 3rd person camera. The only thing added by code is how the camera communicates to the player. We wanted the player control to be relative to the camera (for example, when the player presses the‘ D’ key, they will always move to the right of the camera view). To do that, we had to use some trigonometry to figure out where the camera is looking in relation to the player (Figure 14 below shows the debugging process). It took us some time to figure out the system, but our player feedback over the past few iterations shows that we have been improving and we are satisfied with the final version we have.

For the overall structure, we learned that how Start(), Update() and FixUpdate() functions works in the Unity lifecycle. Because the Update() function executes every frame, it is really important to have a well-formed structure to solve the conflicts in each frame. Everything runs asynchronously. Therefore, it is required to use IEnumnator to allow the codes to run in the order.

  • Stage 1

    • Stage 1 is the simplest stage of all three stages. The goal of the stage is to have players get familiar with the game mechanics. Therefore, it has a simple combination of the raising platform puzzle with pressure plates, a pushable box, gates with their lever triggers, and time-travelling. Time-travelling causes only one difference in this stage, in which the rock blocking the player’s path disappears in the past. In the following stages, time-travelling has more effects and impacts on the game’s environment.

  • Stage 2

    • Stage 2 inherits some mechanics from Stage 1, like raising platforms, boxes, and time-travelling. However, time-travelling causes some different effects in this stage. One is the gap in the center of the stage, which is going to block players to reach the other end of the stage. The other is a slider puzzle that is broken in the present because part of it is on the ground nearby it. It is only working in the past. Besides the difference in time-travelling mechanics, there are two new mechanics. One is the slider puzzle on the wall. The other is the rotating platform which is triggered by the lever trigger.

  • Stage 3

    • Stage 3 looks more complicated, but it is based on the previous two stages. In this stage, the time-travelling mechanic is going to influence the rock again. Rotating platforms, raising platforms, gates with lever triggers, and pushable boxes are all from the last two stages. There are two new mechanics which are the variant of the slider puzzle and rotating platforms. For the slider puzzle variant, this stage contains a rotate puzzle in which players need to rotate the puzzle and form an image. For the rotating platforms variant, four rotating platforms are going to rotate together with one trigger instead of one platform to one trigger.

Reflections

Finally, the most important thing we learned was how to work together as a group on a project. We were quite satisfied with how we all managed to meet twice every week in order to talk about our game and plan out the next steps that we are going to make in the upcoming weeks. Moreover, Github was really important to us in handling the conflicts among our updated versions. In addition, we also use messaging software and apps such as messenger and discord to keep our team updated with all news, changes, and important notices to our team so that no one is left out if there are any new changes made in the game.
